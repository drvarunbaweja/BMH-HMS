What Every Woman in Punjab Should Know

When I started my practice in Ropar after completing my postgraduate training, I encountered something that still troubles me today: women coming to see me for the first time in their third trimester — sometimes just weeks before their due date — having had no antenatal care whatsoever.

Some had never had a single ultrasound. Some had serious conditions like gestational hypertension or gestational diabetes that had gone undetected for months. The reasons were varied — distance to healthcare facilities, financial constraints, family hesitation, cultural beliefs that "women have been having babies naturally for generations." All of these have some truth to them. And yet, in 2025, no woman in Punjab should be facing the risks of childbirth without proper medical support.

This article is my attempt to share — in plain language — what safe pregnancy and delivery actually involves, what warning signs to watch for, and why accessing good gynaecological care isn't a luxury. It is a right.

The Women's Health Landscape in Punjab

Punjab has improved dramatically in terms of maternal health outcomes over the past two decades, and yet significant challenges remain — particularly in semi-urban and rural areas like the districts surrounding Ropar, Anandpur Sahib, Morinda, and Fatehgarh Sahib.

The issues I encounter most frequently in my practice include:

  • Late first antenatal visits — many women first present after 20 weeks
  • Iron deficiency anaemia — affecting more than 50% of pregnant women in Punjab according to NFHS data
  • Gestational diabetes — increasingly common due to dietary patterns and rising obesity rates in the region
  • Hypertension in pregnancy — pre-eclampsia remains a leading cause of maternal mortality nationally
  • Unnecessary C-sections at private facilities on one end, and refusal to consider C-section when genuinely needed at the other
  • Lack of postnatal follow-up — most women disappear after delivery until their next pregnancy

📊 Key Statistic

  • India's Maternal Mortality Ratio (MMR) has fallen from 254 (2004–06) to 97 (2018–20) — a remarkable achievement
  • Punjab's MMR is approximately 105 per 100,000 live births — there is still significant room for improvement
  • Most maternal deaths are preventable with timely access to quality antenatal and intrapartum care

Antenatal Care: Why Every Visit Matters

The World Health Organisation recommends a minimum of 8 antenatal contacts during a normal pregnancy — up from the previous 4-visit model. In my practice, I typically see patients at least once per month through the second trimester, then fortnightly from 28 weeks, and weekly from 36 weeks onwards.

Each visit achieves something specific. It isn't just "checking on the baby" — though that matters enormously. Each visit screens for developing conditions, adjusts supplementation, tracks fetal growth, and builds the trust between doctor and patient that makes a delivery room a calmer, safer place.

What Antenatal Visits Include

  • Blood pressure monitoring — pre-eclampsia screening at every visit
  • Weight monitoring — both under- and over-weight gain carry risks
  • Fundal height measurement — tracking fetal growth
  • Fetal heart rate monitoring — from around 12 weeks onwards
  • Urine dipstick testing — for protein, glucose, and infection
  • Blood tests — haemoglobin, blood group, gestational diabetes screen (OGTT), thyroid function, TORCH infections
  • Anomaly scan at 18–20 weeks — structural screening of the fetus
  • Growth scan at 28–32 weeks and again at 36 weeks
  • Supplementation review — iron, folic acid, calcium, Vitamin D

"Pregnancy is not an illness — but it is a period of significant physiological change that warrants careful, regular monitoring. Catching a problem at an antenatal visit is infinitely better than managing a crisis in the delivery room."

— Dr. Namrata Baweja, MS Gynaecology & Obstetrics

Trimester-by-Trimester Guide

Pregnancy is divided into three trimesters, each with its own developmental milestones and medical priorities. Here is a practical guide to what happens — and what you should be doing — in each phase.

Weeks 1–12

First Trimester

  • Confirm pregnancy with urine test
  • Dating ultrasound (6–8 weeks)
  • NT scan + blood tests (11–13 weeks)
  • Start folic acid 400mcg/day immediately
  • Blood group, Hb, thyroid, HIV, VDRL
  • Manage nausea — small frequent meals
  • Avoid alcohol, tobacco, raw fish
  • Report heavy bleeding immediately
Weeks 13–27

Second Trimester

  • Anomaly scan at 18–20 weeks
  • OGTT for gestational diabetes (24–28 wks)
  • Iron supplementation — anaemia prevention
  • Calcium supplementation begins
  • Feel for fetal movements from ~20 weeks
  • Avoid heavy lifting, contact sports
  • Cervical length scan if risk factors
  • Usually the most comfortable trimester
Weeks 28–40

Third Trimester

  • Growth scans at 28–32 and 36 weeks
  • Discuss birth plan and delivery options
  • Monitor baby's kick counts daily
  • Attend delivery preparation classes
  • Strep B swab at 35–37 weeks
  • NST (Non-stress test) if overdue
  • Hospital bag ready from 36 weeks
  • Know the warning signs (see below)

Normal Delivery vs. C-Section: The Real Story

Few topics in obstetrics generate more emotion — and more misinformation — than the question of how to deliver a baby. I want to be as honest as possible about this, because both extremes do patients a disservice.

The Case for Normal (Vaginal) Delivery

When there are no complications, vaginal delivery is the recommended mode of birth — endorsed by the WHO, FOGSI, and every major obstetric body. The benefits are well-established:

  • Faster recovery — most women are mobile within hours and home within 1–2 days
  • Lower risk of surgical complications (bleeding, infection, blood clots)
  • The newborn receives beneficial bacteria from the birth canal, supporting immune development
  • Better establishment of breastfeeding
  • No surgical scar affecting future pregnancies

We actively support and encourage normal delivery at Baweja Hospital. Our labour ward is designed to be as calm and supportive as possible. Whenever safe, we allow labour to progress naturally.

When C-Section is the Right Choice

But — and this is equally important — there are situations where a caesarean section is not just the right choice, it is the only safe choice. Refusing a C-section when it is medically indicated can have devastating consequences.

Factor Vaginal Delivery Caesarean Section
Uncomplicated pregnancy ✅ Preferred first choice Not indicated without medical reason
Breech presentation Possible but higher risk ✅ Generally recommended
Placenta praevia ❌ Dangerous ✅ Essential
Fetal distress in labour Depends on severity ✅ Emergency C-section if severe
Previous C-section VBAC possible with careful monitoring Often recommended depending on scar type
Pre-eclampsia May be induced vaginally C-section if induction fails or severe
Recovery time 1–2 days typical 3–5 days; 6–8 weeks full recovery

⚠️ Important Note on "On-Demand" C-Sections

There is a growing trend in urban India of C-sections being performed without medical indication — sometimes for convenience, sometimes due to fear of pain. I do not perform C-sections on demand. Every caesarean at Baweja Hospital is performed for a documented medical reason. The risks of unnecessary surgery are real and include future pregnancy complications.

Warning Signs During Pregnancy

Please do not wait for your scheduled appointment if you experience any of the following. These warrant an immediate visit to the hospital or, if severe, a direct call to emergency services.

🚨 Seek Immediate Care if You Experience:

  • Vaginal bleeding at any stage of pregnancy
  • Severe headache that does not respond to paracetamol
  • Visual disturbances — blurred vision, flashing lights, or loss of vision
  • Sudden or severe swelling of face, hands, or feet
  • Severe abdominal pain — persistent, sharp, or cramping
  • Decreased fetal movements — fewer than 10 movements in 2 hours after 28 weeks
  • Fluid leaking from vagina (possible rupture of membranes)
  • High fever — temperature above 38°C
  • Difficulty breathing or chest pain
  • Severe vomiting — unable to keep any food or liquid down

Postnatal Care: The Forgotten Trimester

The period immediately after delivery — the "fourth trimester" as it's increasingly called — deserves much more attention than it typically receives in our setting. The focus shifts almost entirely to the baby, which is understandable. But mothers need care too.

Physical Recovery

  • Perineal care — for tears and episiotomies: keep the area clean, use warm water baths, take prescribed pain relief
  • Caesarean wound care — keep dry for 48 hours, watch for signs of infection (redness, warmth, discharge, fever)
  • Breastfeeding support — initial latch difficulties are common; our nursing team provides hands-on support
  • Postnatal anaemia — iron supplementation should continue for at least 3 months after delivery
  • Contraception — a conversation I have with every patient before discharge; the next pregnancy should be planned, not unplanned

Mental Health

Postnatal blues — a period of emotional vulnerability in the first 1–2 weeks after delivery — affect approximately 50–80% of new mothers. This is different from postnatal depression, which is more persistent, more disabling, and affects around 10–15% of women.

I raise mental health openly with my patients. In our culture, there is still stigma around admitting that motherhood is difficult. But it is. And suffering in silence benefits no one. If you feel persistently low, overwhelmed, anxious, or unable to bond with your baby, please tell someone — and please come and see me.

The 6-Week Postnatal Check

Every woman who delivers at Baweja Hospital is booked for a 6-week postnatal review. This covers: wound healing, blood pressure, anaemia check, contraception planning, breastfeeding review, mental health screening, and — if there were complications in pregnancy — a discussion of implications for future pregnancies.

The Laparoscopic Advantage for Gynaecological Conditions

Pregnancy and delivery are only part of what we do. Many women come to us with gynaecological conditions that have been causing significant distress for years — sometimes decades — without being properly addressed.

Fibroids causing heavy periods. Ovarian cysts causing chronic pain. Endometriosis causing infertility. PCOS affecting quality of life. These conditions are common. They are treatable. And modern laparoscopic surgery means treatment no longer has to mean a large incision, a week in hospital, and weeks off work.

🔬 Laparoscopic Procedures Offered

  • Diagnostic laparoscopy — investigation of unexplained pelvic pain or infertility
  • Laparoscopic myomectomy — fibroid removal with uterine preservation
  • Laparoscopic ovarian cystectomy — cyst removal preserving ovarian tissue
  • Laparoscopic hysterectomy — when required; minimal blood loss, fast recovery
  • Laparoscopic treatment of endometriosis
  • Laparoscopic tubal surgery — for ectopic pregnancy and tubal factor infertility

The benefits compared to open (laparotomy) surgery are significant: smaller incisions, less post-operative pain, shorter hospital stay (typically 1–2 days vs. 4–7 days), faster return to normal activities, and lower risk of adhesion formation affecting future fertility.
