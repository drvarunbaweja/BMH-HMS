Diabetic Eye Disease in Punjab — How to Protect Your Vision Before It's Too Late

April 2026 · 6 min read
Dr. Varun Baweja — UK NHS Cambridgeshire · Baweja Hospital Ropar

Punjab has one of India's highest rates of diabetes — and where diabetes goes, eye disease follows. As an ophthalmologist who trained at UK NHS Cambridgeshire and now practices in Ropar, I see the consequences of uncontrolled diabetes on eyes every single week. This article could save your sight or the sight of someone you love.

How Diabetes Damages Your Eyes

Diabetes damages the tiny blood vessels throughout the body — and the retina (the light-sensitive layer at the back of the eye) has one of the densest concentrations of blood vessels in the entire body. When blood sugar is persistently elevated, these vessels:

  • Become leaky — causing swelling (diabetic macular oedema)
  • Become blocked — causing areas of ischaemia (oxygen starvation)
  • Grow new, fragile vessels (neovascularisation) — which can bleed and scar

This process — diabetic retinopathy — is the leading cause of blindness in working-age adults in India. The tragedy is that it is almost entirely preventable with good sugar control and regular screening.

The Stages of Diabetic Retinopathy

Background (Mild Non-Proliferative)

Early changes visible on retinal examination — micro-aneurysms, small haemorrhages. No vision symptoms. Treatment: improved blood sugar control and annual monitoring.

Moderate to Severe Non-Proliferative

More widespread changes — cotton wool spots, venous changes, IRMA. Some patients develop diabetic macular oedema causing blurred central vision. Treatment: anti-VEGF injections or laser as required.

Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy (PDR)

New blood vessel growth (neovascularisation) — the most dangerous stage. These new vessels are fragile and can bleed into the vitreous, causing sudden severe vision loss. Treatment: pan-retinal photocoagulation laser, anti-VEGF, or vitreoretinal surgery. This is an ophthalmic emergency.

⚠️ Warning signs requiring urgent attention: Sudden floaters, flashing lights, a shadow or curtain across your vision, or sudden blurring — contact us immediately or go to emergency. These may indicate vitreous haemorrhage or retinal detachment.

Diabetes and Glaucoma — The Double Threat

Diabetics are also at significantly higher risk of developing glaucoma. The mechanisms are complex — elevated IOP, changes in optic nerve blood supply, and neovascular glaucoma (new vessel growth on the drainage angle) can all cause glaucoma in diabetic patients.

This is why every diabetic patient should have:

  1. Annual retinal screening (fundus photograph or slit lamp examination)
  2. Annual intraocular pressure (IOP) check
  3. Optic disc assessment for glaucomatous changes

The Golden Rule: Control Your Sugar

No ophthalmological treatment is as effective as good blood sugar control. Studies consistently show that diabetics who maintain HbA1c below 7% have dramatically lower rates of retinopathy and slower progression. Every percentage point of HbA1c reduction translates to significant reduction in retinopathy risk.

  • HbA1c under 7% — optimal
  • HbA1c 7–8% — acceptable with regular monitoring
  • HbA1c over 8% — significant retinopathy risk; requires review

Our general medicine clinic and ophthalmology department work closely together for diabetic patients — you can have your HbA1c checked, see our general physician, and have your eyes examined in the same visit.

Annual Retinal Screening: What's Involved?

A diabetic retinal screen at Baweja Hospital Ropar includes:

  • Dilated fundus examination
  • Retinal photography (fundus camera)
  • Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT) of the macula if indicated
  • IOP measurement
  • Optic disc assessment

Pupils are dilated with drops — the effect lasts 3–4 hours, so bring sunglasses and arrange a driver for the same day.

The Message for Farmers and Rural Punjab

Farming communities in Rupnagar, Morinda, and surrounding districts often have high rates of diabetes — driven by changing diets and reduced physical activity compared to previous generations. Many farmers prioritise work over health — coming in only when symptoms are severe.

By that point, significant and irreversible damage may have occurred. I urge every diabetic patient in our region: don't wait for symptoms. Your eyes may be silently deteriorating right now, and a 30-minute screen could prevent blindness.
