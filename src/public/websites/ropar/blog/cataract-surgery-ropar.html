CATARACT SURGERY

Cataract Surgery in Ropar:
What Patients Need to Know in 2026

By Dr. Varun Baweja April 14, 2026 8 min read

Every day at Baweja Hospital, I meet patients who have been quietly tolerating deteriorating vision for months — sometimes years — because they assumed cataract surgery was a major operation requiring hospitalisation, a long recovery, and significant risk. The truth in 2026 is the precise opposite: modern cataract surgery is one of the safest, most effective, and quickest-recovery procedures in all of medicine. Understanding what it involves, what to expect, and why delaying treatment often causes more harm than good is the purpose of this article.

What Is a Cataract — and What Causes It?

The crystalline lens of the human eye sits just behind the pupil. In a young, healthy eye it is perfectly transparent, allowing light to pass through and focus sharply onto the retina at the back of the eye. A cataract is simply the clouding of this lens. As proteins within the lens denature and clump together over time — a process accelerated by age, ultraviolet light exposure, diabetes, smoking, and certain medications — the lens progressively loses its clarity. The result is vision that appears increasingly foggy, washed-out, or distorted.

Age-related cataract is by far the most common type, typically beginning after the age of 55–60, though many patients in Punjab develop symptoms earlier due to high levels of outdoor ultraviolet exposure, high rates of diabetes, and nutritional factors. Other cataract types include congenital cataracts (present from birth), traumatic cataracts (following eye injury), secondary cataracts (caused by other conditions such as uveitis or prolonged steroid use), and radiation-induced cataracts. The treatment approach for all is fundamentally the same: surgical removal of the clouded lens and replacement with a clear artificial intraocular lens (IOL).

Recognising the Symptoms: When Should You Seek Help?

Cataracts develop gradually, which is why many patients only seek help once vision impairment is already quite significant. The key symptoms to watch for include:

  • Blurred or hazy vision that worsens progressively and cannot be corrected with updated glasses prescriptions
  • Glare and halos around lights, particularly oncoming headlights at night — one of the most disabling early symptoms for drivers
  • Faded or yellowed colour perception — colours appear less vivid and whites take on a yellowish tinge
  • Frequent prescription changes as the cataract shifts the refractive power of the lens, sometimes temporarily improving near vision (the so-called "second sight" phenomenon) before ultimately worsening it
  • Double vision in one eye caused by optical irregularities within the clouding lens
  • Difficulty with near tasks such as reading, threading a needle, or recognising faces in dim light

If you are experiencing any of these symptoms, a comprehensive eye examination at Baweja Hospital will determine whether a cataract is the cause and how far it has progressed. There is no benefit to waiting until vision becomes severely impaired — in fact, very dense cataracts are technically more challenging to remove and carry marginally higher surgical risk than cataracts operated on at an earlier, softer stage.

Modern Cataract Surgery: Phacoemulsification Explained

The gold standard of cataract surgery today is phacoemulsification — a technique that uses ultrasonic energy delivered through a tiny probe to break up and emulsify the clouded lens, which is then aspirated out of the eye through the same micro-incision. The entire procedure is performed through an incision of just 2.2–2.8 mm — so small it is typically self-sealing and requires no sutures. This is not experimental technology: phacoemulsification has been the standard of care in UK NHS hospitals for over two decades, and it is the technique I trained in and continue to use at Baweja Hospital Ropar.

The procedure is performed under topical anaesthesia — eye drops that numb the surface of the eye — so patients remain awake and comfortable throughout. There are no injections around the eye in most cases, no general anaesthesia, and no patch required overnight in the vast majority of patients. The operation itself takes approximately 15–25 minutes per eye, and patients go home the same day, typically within two to three hours of arriving at the hospital.

"The greatest misconception I encounter is that patients must wait until they are 'nearly blind' before having cataract surgery. This is incorrect and potentially harmful. Operate at the right time — not too early, not too late — and outcomes are excellent."

Intraocular Lens (IOL) Choices: Understanding Your Options

Once the clouded natural lens is removed, it must be replaced with an artificial intraocular lens. The IOL choice is one of the most important decisions in cataract surgery, and it is one that patients should make in full understanding of their options and lifestyle needs.

Monofocal IOLs

The standard, most widely used option. A monofocal IOL provides excellent clarity at one fixed focal distance — typically set for distance vision. Patients with monofocal IOLs will generally need reading glasses for near tasks post-operatively. Within this category, aspheric monofocal IOLs offer improved contrast sensitivity over older spherical designs, and toric monofocal IOLs can simultaneously correct pre-existing corneal astigmatism, reducing dependence on distance glasses after surgery.

Extended Depth of Focus (EDOF) IOLs

A newer generation of IOLs that extend the range of clear vision from distance through to intermediate (arm's length — computer screens, dashboards, menus). Most patients with EDOF IOLs find they can manage the vast majority of daily tasks without glasses, though fine near print may still require reading glasses under dim light. EDOF lenses produce fewer optical side effects (halos, glare) than traditional multifocal designs.

Trifocal / Multifocal IOLs

These premium IOLs use diffractive optical zones to provide clear vision at distance, intermediate, and near simultaneously, giving many patients true spectacle independence. They are ideal for patients who strongly wish to be free of glasses post-operatively. A thorough pre-operative assessment — including corneal topography to rule out any surface irregularity — is essential before recommending multifocal IOLs, as they are not suitable for every eye.

Important: IOL Selection Is Personalised

The "best" IOL is not universal — it depends on your lifestyle, the other eye's status, corneal health, pupil size, and your tolerance for optical side effects. Dr. Varun Baweja will discuss all options at your pre-operative assessment and recommend the most appropriate lens for your individual situation.

What to Expect: The Cataract Surgery Journey at Baweja Hospital

Pre-operative assessment: All cataract patients undergo a comprehensive pre-operative workup including visual acuity measurement, slit-lamp biomicroscopy, dilated fundus examination, corneal topography, and biometry (optical measurement of the eye to calculate the precise IOL power needed). This assessment typically takes 60–90 minutes and must be scheduled 1–2 weeks before surgery to allow time for accurate IOL calculation and patient counselling.

Day of surgery: Patients arrive having used the prescribed pre-operative antibiotic drops for three days. Dilating drops are instilled in the eye(s) to be operated on. After a brief wait for dilation to be complete, the patient is taken to the operating theatre. The procedure is performed under topical anaesthesia with the patient relaxed and comfortable. Post-operatively, a protective shield is placed over the eye, and patients rest for 30–45 minutes before being reviewed and discharged with written instructions and a prescription for post-operative drops.

Recovery: Vision begins to improve within 24–48 hours, though full stabilisation may take 4–6 weeks as the eye heals and adapts to the new lens. Mild redness, grittiness, and light sensitivity are normal in the first week. Patients are typically reviewed at day 1, week 1, and 4–6 weeks post-operatively. Most patients can return to light daily activities including reading and watching television the day after surgery. Driving is permitted once vision meets the legal standard (usually 1–2 weeks post-operatively, subject to the surgeon's assessment). Strenuous physical activity and swimming should be avoided for 4 weeks.

Cataract Surgery and Glaucoma: Combined Procedures

Many patients presenting for cataract surgery in the age group most commonly affected (over 55) also have concurrent glaucoma or elevated intraocular pressure. In these patients, combining phacoemulsification with a minimally invasive glaucoma procedure (MIGS) can address both conditions simultaneously — reducing the number of operations required, lowering intraocular pressure, and often reducing dependence on glaucoma eye drops. This combined approach is something I perform routinely at Baweja Hospital and is one of the most meaningful advances in eye surgery for older patients with multiple ocular conditions.

Why Delay Is Harmful: The Case for Timely Surgery

Patients often ask: "Can I wait a little longer?" In some cases — when vision is only mildly affected and does not interfere with daily function — watchful waiting is entirely appropriate. However, there are several compelling reasons not to delay surgery indefinitely once a cataract is visually significant:

  • Dense, mature cataracts are harder to remove. The harder and denser the nucleus of the cataract, the more ultrasonic energy is required during phacoemulsification, which increases the theoretical risk of post-operative corneal swelling and inflammation.
  • Falls and accidents. Impaired vision — particularly difficulty with glare at night — significantly increases the risk of falls, road accidents, and injuries, especially in elderly patients.
  • Quality of life. Vision impairment affects reading, driving, recognising faces, and participating in the social and family activities that matter most. Early, safe surgery restores all of these.
  • Lens-induced complications. Very advanced cataracts can cause secondary glaucoma (phacomorphic or phacolytic), a serious emergency requiring urgent surgery under less ideal conditions.

Frequently Asked Questions About Cataract Surgery

Is cataract surgery painful?

No. The procedure is performed under topical anaesthetic eye drops. Most patients describe mild pressure sensation during the operation but no pain. Any discomfort or grittiness in the first day or two post-operatively is easily managed with the prescribed drops and mild analgesics if needed.

Can both eyes be operated on the same day?

Bilateral simultaneous cataract surgery (BSCS) is practised in some centres under specific protocols. At Baweja Hospital, we generally recommend operating on one eye at a time — separated by 1–2 weeks — to allow assessment of the first eye's outcome and to ensure the second eye benefits from any refinements. This approach minimises risk to both eyes.

Will I need glasses after cataract surgery?

This depends on the IOL type chosen. With a standard monofocal IOL, most patients see well for distance without glasses but will need reading glasses for near tasks. Premium IOLs (EDOF, trifocal) can significantly reduce or eliminate glasses dependence. Discuss your expectations honestly with Dr. Baweja at your pre-operative consultation.

Can a cataract come back after surgery?

The removed cataract cannot recur, as the natural lens has been permanently replaced with the IOL. However, approximately 20–30% of patients develop a "posterior capsule opacity" (PCO) — sometimes called a secondary cataract — months to years after surgery, where the thin membrane behind the IOL becomes cloudy. This is easily treated with a 5-minute, painless in-clinic laser procedure (YAG capsulotomy) that permanently restores vision.

Is cataract surgery covered by insurance in India?

Standard phacoemulsification with a monofocal IOL may be covered by some private health insurance policies, depending on your policy terms. Premium IOLs (multifocal, trifocal) may attract an additional patient-payable component above the insured amount. Our billing team will verify your specific coverage prior to surgery and provide a transparent cost breakdown.
